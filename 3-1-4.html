WCAG 3.1.4: Abbreviations

View main list

Brief summary: A mechanism must be available for identifying the expanded form or meaning of abbreviations.

View the W3C Understanding page for WCAG 3.1.4 Abbreviations.

What SiteLint can test effectively

SiteLint's title-for-abbr rule appears to test one narrow, automatable part of WCAG 3.1.4: explicitly marked <abbr> elements that are missing a non-empty title attribute. This is useful partial coverage because an abbr element with a meaningful title can provide a mechanism for identifying the abbreviation's expanded form.

This rule does not determine whether every abbreviation on the page has been identified, whether the expansion is correct, or whether another valid mechanism is already available nearby, such as an expansion on first use, glossary, footnote, or linked definition.

Current SiteLint coverage expectation

SiteLint reports the abbr element with a missing title attribute. SiteLint does not report the abbr elements that include meaningful title values.

Recommendation for the current rule

Recommendation: Treat this rule as partial WCAG 3.1.4 coverage. It is useful because it catches explicitly marked abbreviations that fail to provide an expansion through title. This page intentionally keeps only the missing-title failure so it isolates the 3.1.4 rule and avoids the separate empty-tooltip rule. It should not be presented as complete 3.1.4 coverage because the SC permits other mechanisms and because many abbreviations are not marked up with abbr at all.

Examples that are not fully covered by SiteLint's current 3.1.4 rule

The following examples are relevant to WCAG 3.1.4, but they are not reliably evaluated by a simple abbr[title] check. Some can be partially detected with heuristics, but full evaluation usually requires understanding the content, surrounding explanation, glossary structure, or whether the abbreviation needs explanation for the intended audience.

Scenario 1: Plain-text abbreviation not marked with abbr

This paragraph uses the abbreviation PDF as plain text. SiteLint's current rule should not report it because there is no abbr element to inspect.

Submit the completed PDF by Friday afternoon.

Recommendation: SiteLint could potentially flag likely all-caps abbreviations as review candidates, but it should not treat every short uppercase word as a confirmed failure. Some abbreviations are common, some are names or acronyms that do not require expansion, and some may already be explained elsewhere.

Scenario 2: Expansion provided in surrounding text instead of abbr title

WCAG 3.1.4 allows mechanisms other than abbr title. In this example, the abbreviation is expanded in the sentence before it appears again.

The Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) protects people with disabilities. The ADA also applies to many public accommodations and government services.

Recommendation: SiteLint should not require abbr title as the only valid mechanism. Detecting whether an abbreviation has already been expanded in nearby prose would require text analysis and still may need human review.

Scenario 3: Abbreviation has a title, but the expansion is wrong

This example uses abbr title, so the current rule should not report it. However, the expansion is intentionally incorrect. A simple rule can confirm that a title exists, but not that it is accurate.

VPAT documents are commonly requested during procurement.

Recommendation: Confirming the correctness of an abbreviation expansion usually requires subject-matter knowledge or content review. Automation may assist with known abbreviation dictionaries, but it cannot reliably validate all domain-specific abbreviations.

Scenario 4: Glossary provides the expansion

This page includes a glossary that defines abbreviations. A glossary can be a valid mechanism, but the current rule only checks individual abbr elements and does not determine whether the glossary covers every abbreviation used in the content.

The audit references ACRs, VPATs, and WCAG throughout the report.

ACR
Accessibility Conformance Report.
VPAT
Voluntary Product Accessibility Template.
WCAG

Recommendation: SiteLint could identify glossary-like structures and abbreviation candidates, but confirming that the glossary is complete and clearly connected to the abbreviations requires content-level judgment.

Scenario 5: Abbreviation may be common enough not to need explanation

Some abbreviations may be widely understood by the intended audience, while others may not. A tool cannot reliably infer audience knowledge from markup alone.

The meeting starts at 10 AM and the room opens at 9:45 AM.

Recommendation: Avoid treating every abbreviation-like token as a failure. The tool can help identify candidates, but a human reviewer may need to decide whether an expansion is necessary for the page's audience and purpose.

Recommended SiteLint treatment

Recommendation: Keep the current title-for-abbr rule as a confirmed automated rule for explicitly marked abbr elements that lack a usable title. Do not present it as complete WCAG 3.1.4 coverage.

Future expansion could include candidate detection for unexplained all-caps abbreviations, glossary detection, and dictionary-assisted validation of common expansions. Those should be framed as review aids or manual-verification candidates unless the tool can confidently identify both the abbreviation and the absence of any valid explanatory mechanism.
